# Project Trace / Project Compass — Baltimore Health Dashboard

## Product Purpose
Transition Baltimore's public health data from a "thermometer" (static visualization) into a "thermostat" (actionable decision-support tool that drives city interventions). The dashboard helps city workers, community organizations, and residents identify where conditions are worsening, where official data contradicts resident experience, and where city spending is or isn't working.

## Register
product

## Users

**Primary — City decision-makers**
Baltimore's Chief Data Officer and department heads who need to cross-reference city expenditures with outcomes. Core question: "We spent money here — did it work?" Time-pressured, data-literate, need specificity not dashboards-for-dashboards-sake.

**Secondary — Community health workers and residents**
Carrollton Ridge pilot community, Digital Navigators (high schoolers and young adults trained as health ambassadors), and community organizations submitting and validating ground-truth reports. Mobile-first, low-bandwidth tolerance required, must feel trustworthy and not patronizing.

**Tertiary — Researchers and journalists**
People using the comparison and share system to document disparities over time. Need exportable, citable views.

## Core features (non-negotiable)

1. **Gap View** — Side-by-side admin/operational data vs. community sentiment (Baltimore Area Survey). Surfaces disconnects. This is the product's primary differentiator.
2. **Multi-level benchmarking** — Every metric shows neighborhood vs. city vs. state vs. federal context simultaneously.
3. **Time slider** — 2016–2023 animated playback for every metric across all 55 CSAs.
4. **Neighborhood comparison** — Pin two neighborhoods (A/B), compare side-by-side across all metrics, share the URL.
5. **Community reporting** — Residents submit conditions; community validates or disputes them. Not merged with official data — always labeled separately.
6. **AI efficacy chatbot** — Natural language querying of the data. CDO use case: "Where did we spend money and are 311 complaints already back?"
7. **Assumptive data layer** — City workers and community members can annotate any metric with qualitative context ("hunches").
8. **Carrollton Ridge pilot mode** — Simplified view (`?pilot=carrollton`) tracking 2 focal issues: illegal dumping and broadband access.

## Tone

Authoritative but human. This is civic infrastructure, not a startup product. No decorative UI, no playfulness. Every element must earn its place. Data labels over clever icons. Precision language. No hedging.

## Anti-references

- Generic civic dashboards that show data without enabling action (ARCGIS public dashboards)
- Pharma/healthcare B2B UIs (white, sterile, intimidating)
- SaaS hero-metric templates (big number, gradient card, dashboard-as-product-marketing)
- Any UI where the map is decoration rather than the primary interaction surface

## Strategic principles

- Data alone is not enough — pair every official metric with its community perception equivalent
- If a feature doesn't help someone decide what to do next, it doesn't belong
- The Carrollton Ridge pilot comes first — prove it works at 1 neighborhood before scaling to 55
- Digital Navigators are first-class contributors, not just users
- Modeled/estimated data must always be visually distinguished from real data

## Brand

City of Baltimore colors: deep navy (#1B4B82), white, and Baltimore orange (#D85A30). Typography should be clear and functional — no decorative serifs on data. Dense information layout is appropriate; this is not a marketing page.
